# Creative Principles

This project is built by someone who believes the best creative work happens when you see clearly, stay connected to the people you make for, take your craft seriously, keep making, and accept that your vision is always evolving. These aren't aspirations. They're how good work actually gets made. Ignore them and the work gets hollow — not as punishment, but as consequence.

These principles are here to help navigate hard decisions, not to prescribe answers.

---

## The Core Structure

**The work speaks first.** Every design choice, every word, every technical decision serves the work itself. If the website is competing with the embroidery for attention, the website is failing. Photography leads. Text supports. Technology enables. Nothing else.

**Make it for someone, not for everyone.** The site exists to connect Wievien's work with people who value handmade craft — not to impress the broadest possible audience. When a choice serves "everyone," it usually serves no one. Know who you're making for.

**Craft is visible in the details.** The same care that goes into a hand-embroidered piece should be felt in the site. Consistent spacing, considered typography, optimized images, thoughtful interactions. Sloppiness in the container undermines trust in the contents.

**Stay in motion.** A portfolio site that stops being updated starts feeling like a memorial. New work, new workshops, new products — the site should feel alive. Every static month is a missed connection.

**Show the process, not just the result.** The journey from thread selection to finished piece is part of the value. Process shots, material choices, technique descriptions — these build connection and demonstrate expertise that finished-product-only presentation can't.

---

## When Choices Are Hard

The principles pull against each other. That tension is the mechanism, not a flaw.

- **Visual impact vs. performance.** Large, beautiful images are the core of the site. But slow-loading pages drive people away before they see the work. Resolution: aggressive image optimization (AVIF/WebP, responsive srcset, lazy loading) so the images are stunning AND fast. Never sacrifice one for the other.

- **Creative expression vs. usability.** The site should feel like a studio, not a template. But experimental navigation or unusual layouts that confuse visitors defeat the purpose. The creativity lives in the visual presentation of the work, not in reinventing how websites work.

- **Dutch authenticity vs. international reach.** Wievien's work is rooted in a specific place and practice. The English version should feel natural, not translated. But the Dutch version is home — that's where the voice is most itself.

- **Showing everything vs. curating.** Every piece matters to the maker. But a portfolio that shows everything equally shows nothing effectively. Curate ruthlessly on the homepage and overview pages. Let the depth live on individual pages.

- **Selling vs. showing.** The site is both exhibition and shop. But pushing sales undermines the gallery experience, and pure exhibition doesn't sustain the practice. Resolution: show beautifully, make buying easy, never make buying loud.

---

## Never Fake the Craft

No stock photography. No AI-generated images of embroidery. No decorative visuals that aren't Wievien's actual work. Every image on the site should be real — real thread, real fabric, real hands, real light. Faking craft in a craft portfolio is the fastest way to destroy the thing you're building.

This extends to words. Don't describe techniques Wievien doesn't use. Don't claim expertise that isn't there. Don't write workshop descriptions that promise more than the workshop delivers. The promise and the reality must match.

---

## What This Means in Practice

When you're unsure which direction to take, ask:

1. Does this choice serve the work — or compete with it?
2. Would someone who values handmade craft feel respected by this experience?
3. Is this authentic to Wievien's actual practice — or is it performing something else?
4. Am I making this for the person who might commission a piece — or for abstract "traffic"?

If the answers point in different directions, sit with the tension. The structure doesn't eliminate hard judgment — it narrows the space to genuinely defensible options.
